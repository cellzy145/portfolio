7 Best Ways to Optimize Your PC for Gaming in 2025

Discover the 7 best ways to optimize your PC for gaming in 2025. Learn how to boost FPS, reduce stuttering, and enhance system performance with practical tips and tools.

Want to squeeze out every last frame from your gaming rig in 2025? Whether you’re on a high-end desktop or a modest laptop, proper PC optimization can make the difference between choppy gameplay and buttery-smooth performance. This guide covers the best and most effective ways to optimize your PC for gaming, from driver updates to system tweaks–even tricks to fix annoying stutters.


We’re skipping vague tips and focusing on actionable steps that actually work in today’s games, especially on Windows 10 and 11. Whether you’re a casual gamer or a competitive player, these tips can help you unlock smoother, faster load times, and a more responsive system.

1. Update Your Graphics Driver

Updating your graphics driver is one of the easiest ways to boost your PC performance, especially in newer games. Driver updates from GPU manufacturers like AMD, Intel, and Nvidia include driver level optimizations and support for new games, as well as bug fixes and possibly new features for your GPU.

What to Do Before Updating Your Graphics Driver

Before updating your drivers, it's important to disable Windows from automatically updating your GPU drivers. This prevents potential conflicts caused by unwanted updates or Windows replacing your custom drivers with generic ones. You can find a guide on how to disable this behavior here.

It is also important to delete the current driver before installing a later version to prevent any instability from driver conflicts. Display Driver Uninstaller is a great tool along with AMD’s AMD Cleanup Utility to remove your current graphics driver while in Safe Mode.

Where to Safely Download GPU Drivers?

GPU drivers are critical pieces of software that act as a bridge between your operating system and your graphics card. They control how your GPU renders graphics, processes workloads, and interacts with your monitor and other hardware.

Because drivers operate at a low level, they have deep access to your system. A faulty or malicious driver can cause crashes, hardware instability, performance degradation — or worse, create security vulnerabilities that expose your system to malware or remote attacks.

You could get safe drivers from downloading from official sources. These sources are:

NVIDIA (GeForce, Quadro, RTX, GTX)

  • Offers both Game Ready Drivers (GRD) and Studio Drivers (SD).

  • You can also use GeForce Experience for auto-updates.

AMD (Radeon RX, Vega, R9, etc.)

  • Choose from Recommended (WHQL) or Optional (Beta) drivers.

  • You can also use AMD Software: Adrenalin Edition (bundled with the driver).

Intel (Arc, Iris, UHD, integrated)

  • It is also good to have Intel Driver & Support Assistant.

Laptop OEMs (Dell, Lenovo, HP, Asus, etc.)

If you have a laptop with custom GPU, especially one with switchable graphics, check:

  • Dell: https://www.dell.com/support

  • HP: https://support.hp.com

  • Lenovo: https://support.lenovo.com

  • Asus: https://www.asus.com/support

Warning: OEM versions may be outdated but are sometimes customized for thermal or power limits—use with caution.

How to Install a Graphics Driver?

After having downloaded the latest compatible driver for your gpu, here is how you install them:

NVIDIA

  • Run the .exe installer.

  • Choose custom installation if you want a clean install (recommended).

  • Follow the prompts and restart your PC.

Alternatively, you can install it via GeForce Experience*.

AMD

  • Run the .exe installer.

  • Choose Factory reset for a clean install (optional, but recommended).

  • Let it complete, then restart.

Also installs AMD Software: Adrenalin Edition for monitoring and tuning. You could also install the latest driver directly from it if you already have it beforehand*.

Intel

  • Run the .exe installer.

  • Follow the on-screen instructions.

  • Restart your PC when prompted.

For laptops, use the DCH (Driver Componentized Hardware) version if you’re unsure.

*Do note that this method bypasses the clean-install process but may still work reliably if no issues are present.

2. Use a High Performance Power Plan

Switching to a High Performance power plan can help ensure that your CPU and GPU deliver maximum performance without being held back by aggressive power-saving features. This is especially noticeable in CPUs that dynamically adjust their clock speeds based on workload.

By default, Windows often uses the Balanced plan, which prioritizes energy efficiency. While that’s fine for most users, gamers and power users may benefit from setting their PC to High Performance or even Ultimate Performance (on supported systems) to reduce latency and avoid clock speed fluctuations.

How Does a High Performance Power Plan Help?

  • Reduces CPU latency: Keeps the CPU clock speed higher for faster responsiveness.
  • Prevents downclocking: The CPU doesn’t idle as often, reducing performance dips in fast-paced games.
  • Helps older systems: If your CPU is older or inconsistent in performance, this can help smooth things out.

Note: For modern desktop systems with good cooling, the increase in power usage is usually negligible while gaming. However, on laptops, it can significantly impact battery life and heat output.

How to Enable High Performance Power Plan

Windows 10/11

  1. Press Windows + R, type powercfg.cpl, and press Enter.
  2. In the Control Panel window, select High Performance.
    • If it’s missing, click “Show additional plans.”
  3. To enable Ultimate Performance (for enthusiast desktops):
    • Open Command Prompt as Administrator.
    • Run: powercfg -duplicatescheme e9a42b02-d5df-448d-aa00-03f14749eb61
    • Go back to the Power Options window, and select Ultimate Performance.

Why High Performance Plans Are Not Recommended for Ryzen X3D CPU Users

If you’re using any AMD Ryzen X3D processor, such as the 5800X3D, 7800X3D, or 9800X3D, avoid switching to High Performance or Ultimate Performance plans.

These CPUs are designed to work best with AMD’s Balanced Power Plan, which:

  • Enables proper core parking, focusing workload on the right cores.
  • Helps with thermal management for the 3D V-Cache layer.
  • Ensures Windows schedules game threads on the correct CCD (for multi-CCD CPUs like 7950X3D).

Using any other plan may hurt game scheduling efficiency and thermals on these CPUs.

Recommended: Install the AMD Chipset Drivers to unlock the AMD-optimized Balanced plan for your system.

3. Disable Unnecessary Startup Programs

Disabling unnecessary startup programs is a simple way to make your PC boot faster and perform better during games. Many background apps launch automatically when your system starts — using up RAM, CPU power, and even affecting your frame rates without you realizing it.

Benefits of Disabling Unnecessary Startup Apps

  • Faster Boot Times: This is the most immediately noticeable benefit. By preventing unnecessary applications from launching at startup, Windows – as well as other operating systems – has fewer processes to initiate, thereby leading to a significantly faster boot time.
  • Improved System Responsiveness: With fewer apps running in the background, your system can dedicate more resources to gaming or whatever you’re actively working on.
  • Reduced Resource Consumption: Many startup apps – like updaters, sync clients, or launchers – use memory and CPU in the background, which can impact game performance.
  • Less Disk Activity: Some apps perform background indexing, syncing, or checks. Disabling them can reduce disk I/O, helping games load faster and reduce stutters.
  • Potentially Improved Battery Life (Laptops): Fewer background tasks mean your laptop isn’t burning power on things you’re not using.
  • Less Cluttered System Tray: Trimming startup apps also reduces visual clutter, giving you a cleaner, distraction-free taskbar.

How to Disable Startup Apps in Windows

  1. Open Task Manager
    Press Ctrl + Shift + Esc or right-click the taskbar and choose Task Manager.

  2. Go to the Startup Tab
    This shows all apps set to run at startup, along with their impact settings.

  3. Disable Unneeded Apps
    Right-click and hit Disable on apps you don’t need immediately.

    Common examples:

    • Game launchers (Epic, EA, Battle.net – unless you use them every boot)
    • Cloud services like OneDrive or DropBox (if not needed instantly)
    • Updaters (Java, Adobe, Logitech, etc.)

Note: Keep essential items like audio drivers, antivirus, and system services enabled.

Advanced Startup Control (Optional)

Power users may prefer tools like Autoruns for deeper control of background processes that don’t appear in Task Manager. Autoruns can reveal hidden services, drivers, and scheduled tasks not visible in Task Manager – ideal for tech-savvy users.

4. Tweak Windows Settings for Better Game Performance

Although these Windows tweaks won’t give you dramatic boosts in FPS, they can help improve consistency and allow the game to run stably.

Disable Xbox Game Bar and Captures

Disabling the Xbox Game Bar and background recording prevents unnecessary CPU and disk usage. It is especially useful on systems with low RAM or without SSDs.

To disable it:

  • Go to Settings
  • Click on Gaming
  • Select Xbox Game Bar and Captures
  • Toggle both features off

Enable Hardware-Accelerated GPU Scheduling (HAGS)

Enabling HAGS can reduce latency and improve performance slightly on newer GPUs (NVIDIA 10-series and above, AMD RDNA/RDNA2+).
Results may vary — in some cases, it can introduce stutters depending on the game and drivers.

To enable it:

  • Go to Settings
  • Click on System
  • Navigate to Display
  • Select Graphics
  • Click on Default Graphics Settings
  • Toggle Hardware-Accelerated GPU Scheduling on

5. Manage Background Processes with Free Tools

Even after disabling startup apps, many hidden background processes still run and take up valuable system resources. Free tools can help you identify and control these processes more effectively — giving your PC extra breathing room for gaming.

Why Manage Background Processes?

  • Free up RAM and CPU: More resources for your game, fewer slowdowns.
  • Reduce background disk usage: Helps avoid stuttering and faster game loading.
  • Improve responsiveness: Less lag when alt-tabbing or opening in-game overlays.

Recommended Free Tools

  • Microsoft Autoruns

    • Shows everything that starts with Windows, including hidden tasks and drivers.

    • Great for advanced users who want full control.

    • Tip: Focus on the Logon, Scheduled Tasks, and Services tabs.

    • ⚠️ Be careful! Disabling critical services can cause issues. Research each entry before changing it.

  • MSI Afterburner + Rivatuner Statistics Server

    • While mainly used for monitoring, it lets you track CPU/GPU usage, temperatures, and more.

    • Identifies background apps spiking resource usage during gaming sessions.

    • Not a cleaner, but a diagnostic tool to spot misbehaving background processes.

  • Process Explorer

    • Think of it as an advanced Task Manager.

    • See which processes are using the most resources in real time.

    • You can suspend or terminate tasks that are hogging performance (just don’t close system-critical processes).

  • Debloaters

    • Tools like ThisIsWin11, O&O ShutUp10++, or BloatyNosy can help disable telemetry, background services, and unnecessary features in Windows.

    • Can significantly reduce background activity on clean installs.

    • ⚠️ Use with caution and back up your system before applying any batch debloat scripts.

When Should You Use These?

  • If your Task Manager shows high CPU, memory, or disk usage at idle.

  • If your game stutters despite having decent hardware.

  • If you want to squeeze every bit of performance from your system without buying upgrades.

6. Fix Game Stuttering by Disabling MPO (MultiPlane Overlay)

Multiplane Overlay (MPO) is a Windows feature meant to improve performance and reduce power consumption by allowing parts of the screen to be composited separately. However, in practice, it’s been linked to severe frame drops, flickering, and inconsistent frame pacing — especially on systems using G-Sync or FreeSync.

Why Disabling MPO Can Help

  • Fixes microstuttering in certain games (especially borderless fullscreen).
  • Resolves screen flickering issues in some multi-monitor or VR setups.
  • Improves frame pacing for smoother motion in fast-paced games.

If you’ve tried everything else and still experience occasional stutters — even on capable hardware — MPO might be the hidden culprit.

How to Disable MPO in Windows

⚠️ Caution: This requires a quick registry tweak. Back up your registry before proceeding.

  1. Press Windows + R, type regedit, and hit Enter.
  2. Navigate to:
    HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\Dwm
  3. Right-click in the right panel → New → DWORD (32-bit) Value
  4. Name it: OverlayTestMode
  5. Set the value to: 5
  6. Restart your PC.

This disables MPO system-wide.

How to Re-enable MPO (if needed)

Simply delete the OverlayTestMode entry or set its value to 0, then restart.

Additional Notes

  • This tweak is reversible and safe if done correctly.
  • Especially helpful for NVIDIA and AMD GPU users running games in borderless fullscreen.
  • Doesn’t significantly impact battery life or performance for most modern systems.

7. Optimize In-Game Settings for Smooth Performance

No matter how powerful your PC is, optimizing in-game settings can dramatically improve your performance and responsiveness — especially in newer, more demanding titles. It’s also the most direct way to tailor visuals and frame rates to your preferences and hardware.

Why Tweaking In-Game Settings Matters

  • Boosts FPS by offloading GPU-intensive effects.
  • Reduces input lag and improves responsiveness.
  • Improves consistency — smoother frame pacing means fewer stutters.
  • Adapts to your hardware — you don’t need to max everything out.

Even high-end rigs benefit from tuning settings per game, especially when aiming for high refresh rates like 120Hz or 144Hz.

Which Settings to Adjust First

Setting Impact on Performance Recommendation
Resolution Very High Use native for clarity; drop for big gains
Anti-Aliasing (AA) High Use TAA or FXAA; avoid MSAA on low-end GPUs
Shadow Quality High Lower it — big FPS gain with minor quality loss
Post-Processing Effects Moderate Disable motion blur, film grain, chromatic aberration
Ambient Occlusion High Try SSAO or turn off completely for more FPS
Ray Tracing Very High Disable unless you have a high-end GPU
V-Sync Varies Use G-Sync/FreeSync or a frame limiter instead

Some settings have a much larger impact on performance than others.
Focus on reducing GPU-heavy effects first. Lowering just 2–3 of these can result in significant performance gains.

Frame Rate Targets (FPS Benchmarks)

If your game includes a built-in benchmark, use it to test setting changes before jumping in.

Pro Tip: Use Presets, Then Fine-Tune

Most games offer Low–Ultra presets. Start with the one closest to your goal FPS, then tweak individual settings for better balance. For example:

  • Begin with "High" preset
  • Lower Shadows and Ambient Occlusion
  • Turn off Motion Blur and V-Sync

Optional: Use Tools Like NVIDIA/AMD Game Optimizers

GeForce Experience (NVIDIA) and AMD Software: Adrenalin Edition both offer automatic tuning based on your system specs. These can be helpful starting points, especially if you’re unsure where to begin.

Gaming in 2025 demands more from your hardware than ever–but optimizing your PC doesn’t mean you need to upgrade right away. By keeping your drivers up to date, adjusting system settings, and minimizing background distractions, you can extend the life of you current setup and enjoy better performance across the board.

From power plans to in-game settings, these optimizations can help you get the most out of your PC–whether you’re aiming for ultra-low latency in esports titles or simply trying to make your system feel snappier overall.

Remember: Optimization isn’t about chasing perfection–it’s about finding the balance between performance, stability, and personal preference.
